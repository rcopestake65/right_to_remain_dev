Welfare Interview

Claiming asylum means asking the UK for protection because you fear for your life (you are scared) for very specific reasons under the law.

To claim asylum, you either tell the immigration officials (government workers) at the airport/port you arrive at, or you go to a Home Office building if you have not claimed asylum at the airport/port.

The Home Office is a government department dealing with immigration and asylum. Home Office case workers are the people who will look into your reasons for wanting protection and stay in the UK.

You will usually need to go to Home Office in Croydon if you don't claim asylum at the airport/port, but if you are a long way from Croydon in South London, the Home Office may allow you to go to an office nearer to where you are.

Problems & Actions

Problem 1

You don't know how to claim asylum

Action 1

Go to the police or ask someone to show you how to get to the local council or social services

Problem 2

You turn up at the Home Office to claim asylum without an appointment.

Action 2

You explain that you are homeless, and that the Home Office policy is that “You don’t need to make an appointment if you have nowhere to live – you can go to the screening unit between 7:30am and 4:00pm, Monday to Friday”

Problem 3

You claim asylum a week or two after arrival. Not everyone will claim asylum as soon as they arrive, and there can be good reasons for this. However, it is really important you claim as soon as possible because this could go against you in your application for asylum. The Home Office may argue that this is evidence that you are not in real danger.

Action 3

If you don’t claim asylum immediately on entering the UK, you will need to telephone to make an appointment to claim asylum at the Screening Unit in Croydon.

Action 3

If you don’t claim asylum immediately on entering the UK, you will need to telephone to make an appointment to claim asylum at the Screening Unit in Croydon. You can do so by searching “Asylum Screening Unit Croydon” online and calling the number. You may have to try many times before you get through.

You can ask your solicitor to explain to the Home Office why you didn’t claim asylum straight away.

Problem 4

You do not have a solicitor when you claim asylum.

Action 4

If possible, you should claim with a solicitor - but this is not always possible early on in the asylum process. Your local authority social worker will help you with finding a solicitor.

Problem 5

The Home Office does not believe you are under 18 and say that you will be moved to (dispersed) initial adult asylum accommodation.

Action 5

If there are any issues regarding age, you can ask you advocate to support you, get referred to the Refugee Council age dispute project, or to a community care solicitor for advice and representation.

Action 5

If there are any issues regarding age, you can ask you advocate to support you, get referred to the Refugee Council age dispute project, or to a community care solicitor for advice and representation.

The law says that if you do not have any papers (documents) proving your age, the Home office can only say that you are over 18 if the way you look and behave strongly suggests you are 25 years or over.

  • If sent to adult accommodation during this screening interview, you can start an age dispute with the help of the refugee council (for example). This means you can disagree with the home office decision about your age and ask someone to help you be recognised as a child
  • This will trigger a request from the Home Office to the LA for an age assessment- this means social services will be asked to determine your age through a series of questions about your life. They will also ask about your behaviour to people who know you.
  • Social services do not have to do a full assessment just because the Home Office ask them to do so. They should give you the benefit of the doubt (believe you are a child) unless they strongly believe you are significantly over 18.
  • While you are being age assessed, you will be placed under local authority care (with social services) and should be treated as a child. This means you will benefit from the same entitlements as any other child in care such as suitable accommodation, food, clothing, access to education and healthcare, and support from a social worker, foster carer or key worker.
  • The age assessment should be completed within 28 days and social services should give you an answer then (although it can significantly longer). You may be requested to attend several meetings where you will be asked a lot of questions about your life. This is because social services want to determine a chronology (timeline) of your life and understand if your behaviour corresponds to that of a child or young person under 18.
  • During all these meetings, someone who does not work for social services will be there to support you. This person is called an Appropriate Adult.

Problem 6

It's been a month since you've had your welfare interview and you have not received your ARC card.

Action 6

Your card should be with you within three days after your welfare interview. It is usually sent out to your social worker. Check with your social worker or ask you foster carer/key worker to check with your social worker. If this hasn't arrived within a week, ask your social worker or your solicitor to contact the Home Office.

Problem 7

You have experienced some very upsetting things. You may have been trafficked, experienced sexual violence, domestic violence or torture.

You are scared to tell the Home Office this.

Action 7

If one of these things has happened to you, it is a good idea to say so in your welfare interview.

Read More

Action 7

If one of these things has happened to you, it is a good idea to say so in your welfare interview. You will not be required to discuss what has happened to you in detail at this stage. Talk to someone you trust about how best to tell the Home Office what has happened (your solicitor might write a statement using your words so you don’t have to say the words to the Home Office in an interview).

If there are worries that you may have been trafficked, you will be referred onto the “National Referral Mechanism” (NRM) by the Home Office or the Local Authority. This means they will try to get additional support for you.
